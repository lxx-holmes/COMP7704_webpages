Profitability Analysis

- Net Profit Margin declined from 27.45% (2021) to 16.48% (2022) due to increased operating expenses (rising from $6,224.9M to $8,358.8M) and $655.9M impairment charges, outpacing revenue growth.
- Operating Profit Margin dropped from 33.48% to 21.97%, driven by higher SG&A expenses ($811.6M to $972.3M) and depreciation/amortization costs ($2,332.6M to $3,355.1M).
- Return on Assets (ROA) fell from 3.67% to 2.63%, reflecting reduced asset efficiency as expense growth eroded net income.
- Return on Equity (ROE) decreased sharply from 28.30% to 14.23%, signaling lower returns for shareholders amid rising costs.
- Profitability declines were exacerbated by revenue growth being outpaced by operating expense increases, SG&A expansion, and non-cash impairment charges.
- All key ratios (margins, ROA, ROE) showed deterioration, highlighting systemic challenges in cost management and operational efficiency.

- Cash ratio rose from 0.215 to 0.244 due to $78.5M cash increase, but low ratio highlights ongoing dependence on non-cash assets to meet obligations.
- Reduced current liabilities by $755.4M (2021-2022) and strategic debt repayments (e.g., U.S. Subsidiary Debt) enhanced all liquidity ratios.
- Working capital management improvements noted in financial reports contributed to slight liquidity gains despite persistent sub-1 ratios.

- Times Interest Earned Ratio mirrored the Interest Coverage Ratio decline, reflecting reduced earnings capacity relative to interest obligations due to higher variable-rate debt costs.
- Equity growth was driven by strategic capital allocation, including debt repayment and equity issuance, enhancing financial stability despite increased leverage risks.
- Debt restructuring (e.g., 3.650% and 4.050% senior unsecured notes) optimized capital structure but exposed the company to elevated financial risk from interest expense pressures.

- Lower DSO reduces working capital needs and enhances liquidity, while reduced DPO optimizes cash usage but risks supplier relationship strain.
- The CCC improvement reflects strategic cash management evolution, balancing receivables/payables dynamics despite tighter cash flow conditions.
- Negative CCC in 2021 highlighted exceptional working capital efficiency, whereas 2022's positive CCC signals adjusted but still effective operational timing.
- Changes are driven by enhanced collection practices, cash reserve growth, and payment term optimization, aligning with AMT's working capital prioritization.
