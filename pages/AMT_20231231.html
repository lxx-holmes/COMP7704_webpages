Profitability Analysis

- Net Profit Margin decreased from 16.5% (2022) to 13.3% (2023) due to $173.7 million in impairment charges, higher interest expenses, and foreign currency fluctuations outweighing operational gains.
- Operating Profit Margin rose from 22.0% to 27.1%, driven by higher revenues, lower direct expenses in regions (e.g., U.S. & Canada), and foreign currency benefits in Asia-Pacific.
- ROA declined from 2.63% to 2.25% as total assets decreased slightly (from $67,194.5M to $66,027.6M) without proportional income growth, indicating less efficient asset utilization.
- ROE dropped marginally from 14.2% to 13.7% due to reduced net income from non-operating costs outpacing the slight decrease in equity.
- ROIC improved from 3.62% to 4.72% reflecting better capital efficiency from higher operating profit and cost management across segments.
- Operational efficiency gains included increased gross margins in U.S. & Canada (revenue growth, lower expenses) and Asia-Pacific (currency effects).
- Non-operating expenses rose sharply due to interest costs and $173.7 million in impairment charges, eroding net profitability.
- Asset utilization challenges emerged despite lower total assets, as income growth lagged asset changes.
- Equity efficiency dipped slightly as net income decline offset equity reduction.
- Debt increase contributed to higher interest expenses, highlighting a need for debt management to improve profitability.

Liquidity Analysis

- Current Ratio improved from 0.44 (2022) to 0.51 (2023) due to decreased current liabilities ($8,311.9M to $7,249.3M), but remains below 1, indicating potential short-term liquidity challenges.
- Quick Ratio increased slightly from 0.33 to 0.36, supported by better receivables management despite a minor cash decrease (2023: $1,973.3M vs. 2022: $2,028.4M), yet still below 1.
- Cash Ratio rose from 0.24 to 0.27 primarily from reduced current liabilities, though cash equivalents slightly declined (2023: $1,973.3M vs. 2022: $2,028.4M).
- Key factors: Current liabilities decreased by $1,062.6M (2022-2023), operating income increased enhancing liquidity despite lower net income, and improved receivables management contributed to Quick Ratio growth.
- All liquidity ratios remain below 1, signaling persistent short-term liquidity risks requiring management focus on increasing current assets or further reducing liabilities for financial stability.

Solvency Analysis

- Debt-to-Equity ratio increased from 4.42x (2022) to 5.08x (2023) due to new borrowings including a 10B INR India Term Loan, Nigeria Letters of Credit, and finance leases, raising financial leverage and risk.
- Interest Coverage Ratio improved slightly from 2.13x to 2.27x, driven by marginally higher EBIT, but remains low and vulnerable to earnings volatility.
- Solvency Ratio (total liabilities/equity) rose from 4.42x to 5.08x, reflecting increased debt reliance through term loans and subsidiary obligations, signaling riskier financial positioning.
- Working capital facilities provided 10.7B INR borrowing capacity (0.2B INR utilized for guarantees), adding liquidity while increasing debt exposure.
- Amendments to debt agreements, including extending the India Term Loan maturity to December 2024, eased repayment timing without reducing total debt burden.
- Compliance with debt covenants remains critical to avoid accelerated repayment risks, requiring disciplined debt management amid elevated leverage.
- Low interest coverage (2.27x) and high solvency ratios highlight sensitivity to operational performance declines, necessitating cautious capital structure optimization.

Efficiency Analysis

- DSO improved to 21.96 days in 2023 (vs. 25.83 in 2022) due to enhanced receivables collection processes, including stricter creditworthiness monitoring and allowance adjustments for doubtful accounts.
- DPO increased to 29.46 days in 2023 (vs. 25.27 in 2022) as the company negotiated longer supplier payment terms, delaying cash outflows.
- Cash Conversion Cycle shifted from 0.56 days (2022) to -7.5 days (2023), reflecting faster customer collections than supplier payments, optimizing working capital efficiency.
- Negative CCC indicates cash is collected from customers before supplier payments are due, improving liquidity for growth initiatives.
- Efficiency gains stem from active management of receivables (reduced credit risk) and strategic extension of payables, supported by financial statement disclosures on working capital optimization.
- The 3.87-day DSO reduction and 4.19-day DPO increase collectively drove the CCC improvement, demonstrating stronger cash flow management.
