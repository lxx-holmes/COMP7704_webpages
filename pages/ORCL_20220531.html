üöÄ Business Development

- Oracle shifted focus to more profitable Oracle Engineered Systems and strategic hardware products while de-emphasizing lower-margin commodity hardware, aiming to optimize profitability amid declining hardware revenues.
- Acquired Cerner Corporation in June 2022, expanding into healthcare IT solutions and positioning Oracle to integrate digital health systems with its cloud infrastructure.
- Increased investment in cloud services and license support, driving growth in cloud revenues despite global supply chain disruptions and pandemic-related challenges.
- Implemented workforce restructuring and hybrid work policies to enhance flexibility, retain talent, and reduce costs, while addressing productivity and cybersecurity risks in remote environments.
- Expanded cloud infrastructure (OCI) offerings, including Oracle Autonomous Database and MySQL HeatWave, leveraging AI/ML to improve performance and automation for enterprise clients.
- Prioritized R&D spending ($7.2B in FY2022) to advance cloud technologies, enhance SaaS applications (e.g., Fusion Cloud ERP, HCM), and integrate emerging tools like IoT and blockchain.
- Strengthened indirect sales channels through partnerships with resellers, system integrators, and ISVs to drive cloud and license adoption, despite risks from channel partner dependencies.
- Addressed supply chain shortages by increasing inventory reserves and securing manufacturing capacity, though higher costs impacted margins and cash flows.
- Grew services business revenue by 6% YoY (constant currency), driven by consulting and customer support in Americas and EMEA regions, offsetting declines in Asia Pacific.
- Launched workforce retention strategies, including equity-based compensation and global hiring in lower-cost regions, amid competitive talent markets and rising compensation pressures.

Risks:

- Continued R&D investments ($7.2B annually) fuel innovation in enterprise software and cloud technologies.

üìä Financials

Key Items

- Acquisition-related and operating expenses rose significantly as a percentage of revenue, driving a 28.1% operating income decline and 51.1% net income drop year-over-year.
- Operating income and net income as a percentage of revenue decreased sharply, with non-operating expenses becoming material and income tax provision shifting from positive to negative.
- Cash and equivalents fell from 22.9% to 19.5% of total assets, while marketable securities plummeted from 12.5% to 0.5%, indicating reduced liquidity.
- Property, plant, and equipment increased from 5.4% to 8.9% of assets, signaling capital investment, while intangible assets declined from 1.9% to 1.3%.
- Current notes payable decreased from 6.3% to 3.4% of liabilities, though accounts payable doubled from 0.6% to 1.2%.
- Shareholders' equity deteriorated sharply from 4.0% to -5.7% of total liabilities/equity, with total equity declining 96.7% year-over-year.
- Total assets decreased by 16.8%, reflecting portfolio rebalancing and reduced marketable securities holdings.

Profitability Analysis

- Net profit margin declined from 33.9% (2021) to 15.8% (2022) due to 51% net income drop ($13,746M to $6,717M) and 24.7% operating expense increase.
- Operating profit margin fell from 37.6% to 25.7%, driven by higher R&D, sales costs, and stock-based compensation.
- ROA decreased from 10.5% to 6.1%, reflecting reduced asset efficiency despite a 16.6% smaller asset base ($109,297M in 2022).
- ROE turned negative (-116.5% in 2022 vs. 231% in 2021) due to negative equity from $28.2B Cerner acquisition increasing liabilities.
- Total equity shifted to negative, indicating financial strain from acquisition-related debt and integration costs.
- Revenue recognition timing (particularly cloud services) and foreign exchange fluctuations further pressured profitability.
- Operational inefficiencies and currency impacts contributed to lower net income, highlighting strategic adjustment needs.

Liquidity Analysis

- Current Ratio declined to 1.62x in 2022 ($31.6B current assets / $19.5B liabilities) from 2.30x in 2021 ($55.6B / $24.2B), driven by $3.8B long-term debt reclassified as current liabilities and $16.2B stock repurchases.
- Cash Ratio fell to 1.10x in 2022 ($21.4B cash / $19.5B liabilities) from 1.24x in 2021 ($30.1B / $24.2B) due to $16.2B stock buybacks and $4.7B litigation charges reducing cash reserves.
- Quick Ratio remained incalculable both years due to missing inventory data disclosure.
- Operating cash flow dropped 40% YoY, limiting liquidity buffer for obligations.
- Current liabilities rose 23% in 2022, partially from $3.8B debt reclassification reducing long-term debt.
- Liquidity erosion reflects cash-intensive activities (acquisitions, buybacks) outweighing operational cash generation.

Solvency Analysis

- Debt-to-Equity ratio turned negative in 2022 due to liabilities exceeding assets (-$5,768 equity), worsened by $16.2 billion stock repurchases and increased borrowings (2021 ratio: 21.05).
- Interest Coverage ratio declined from 6.10 (2021) to 3.96 (2022) due to higher interest expenses from $15 billion senior notes issued in 2021 and lower EBIT ($10,926 vs. $15,213).
- Equity Ratio dropped from 4.5% (2021) to negative (2022) primarily from stock buybacks, $1.692 billion unrealized losses on securities, and impairment charges eroding equity.
- Total debt increased with $15 billion senior notes issued in March 2021, partially offset by $8.3 billion repayments in 2022.
- Cash and marketable securities fell 53% to $21.9 billion in 2022, reducing liquidity for obligations and growth investments.
- Negative equity position reflects high financial leverage, with total liabilities at $115,065 (2022) versus $109,297 total assets.
- Declining EBIT combined with rising interest expenses ($2,755 in 2022 vs. $2,496 in 2021) reduced debt service capacity.
- Share repurchases totaling $16.2 billion in 2022 directly reduced equity, exacerbating solvency risks.
- Comprehensive losses and investment impairments further weakened the equity base, signaling long-term financial stability concerns.

Efficiency Analysis

- Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) increased from 49 days (2021) to 51 days (2022), indicating slower accounts receivable collections.
- Hardware revenue declines and customer financial struggles likely delayed payments, contributing to higher DSO.
- Workforce restructuring disrupted operational efficiency in accounts receivable management processes.
- Reliance on indirect sales channels (resellers, integrators) extended payment cycles due to intermediary payment terms.
- COVID-19 supply chain disruptions may have indirectly caused customer payment delays.
- Inventory and COGS data absence prevented Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) and Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) calculations.
- Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) analysis remained incomplete for 2021 and 2022 due to missing DIO/DPO metrics.
- Accounts receivable management improvements are critical to address collection inefficiencies highlighted by rising DSO.
