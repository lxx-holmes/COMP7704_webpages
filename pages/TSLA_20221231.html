- Income before taxes increased 5.05% as a percentage of total revenue.

Profitability Analysis

- Gross profit margin improved slightly from 25.3% (2021) to 25.6% (2022) due to pricing adjustments, $365 million sales return reserve release in 2021, and sustained cost control measures.
- Operating profit margin increased from 12.1% to 16.8%, driven by a $571 million (13%) reduction in SG&A expenses, including lower stock-based compensation and employee-related costs.
- Net profit margin rose from 10.3% to 15.4%, supported by 430% higher interest income, 49% lower interest expense, and reserve releases boosting automotive revenue.
- ROA improved from 8.9% to 15.3% as revenue grew 51% ($53.8B to $81.5B) and operational efficiencies enhanced asset utilization in manufacturing.
- ROE surged from 18.3% to 28.1% due to net income doubling ($5.5B to $12.6B), reserve releases, and potential impacts from stock splits/buybacks noted in financial statements.
- Total revenue increased 51% year-over-year, driven by higher automotive sales/leasing and operational scale effects.
- Expense reductions included $571M in SG&A savings and 49% lower interest expenses, improving overall cost structure.
- Manufacturing efficiency gains and operational streamlining contributed to better asset productivity and profitability ratios.

Liquidity Analysis

- Current Ratio improved from 1.37 (2021) to 1.53 (2022) as current assets ($27.1B to $40.9B) grew faster than liabilities ($19.7B to $26.7B), driven by inventory doubling and higher receivables/prepaid expenses, enhancing liquidity.
- Quick Ratio declined slightly from 1.08 to 1.05 due to increased reliance on inventory (excluded from the ratio), tempering liquidity improvement despite overall asset growth.
- Cash Ratio dropped from 0.89 to 0.61 as cash reserves decreased ($17.6B to $16.3B) amid rising liabilities, likely due to investments in property/equipment, reducing immediate cash coverage of obligations.
- Inventory more than doubled during the period, contributing to current ratio strength but reducing the proportion of highly liquid assets in quick ratio calculations.
- Accounts receivable and prepaid expenses increased substantially, further boosting current assets but not directly improving cash-based liquidity metrics.
- Reduced cash reserves highlight potential short-term flexibility risks despite stronger overall liquidity from asset management improvements.

Solvency Analysis

- Debt-to-Equity ratio decreased from 1.012 (2021) to 0.816 (2022) due to $819M debt repayment (including Solar Asset Notes), $29M debt-to-equity conversion, and equity growth from stock issuances/retained earnings.
- Interest Coverage Ratio surged from 17.58 (2021) to 71.45 (2022) as EBIT doubled to $13,656M and interest expenses fell to $191M (from $371M in 2021).
- Debt extinguishment charges in 2022 increased short-term costs but reduced long-term debt obligations, contributing to solvency improvement.
- EBIT growth (+109% YoY) and reduced leverage reflect strategic focus on profitability and debt reduction, strengthening long-term financial stability.
- Equity position improved via retained earnings and capital raises, lowering reliance on debt financing.

Efficiency Analysis

- Tesla's Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) shifted from -21 days in 2021 to 0 days in 2022, indicating reduced efficiency in converting inventory to cash due to slower inventory turnover.
- DSO increased from 10 to 12 days (2021 vs. 2022), reflecting slower collections as accounts receivable rose from $1,913 million to $2,952 million.
- DIO surged from 43 to 64 days, driven by higher inventory levels ($5,757 million in 2021 to $12,839 million in 2022) and early production of new models like the Tesla Semi.
- DPO increased slightly from 74 to 76 days, with accounts payable growing from $10,025 million to $15,255 million, indicating marginally extended supplier payment terms.
- The negative CCC in 2021 signaled faster cash generation than usage, while the 2022 neutral CCC reflects balanced cash flows amid inventory buildup.
- Slower inventory turnover (21-day DIO increase) was the primary driver of the CCC deterioration, outweighing minor changes in receivables and payables.
